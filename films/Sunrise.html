Waiting for Sunrise

A love story about guilt & existential crises

FOLLOWING HIS DIVORCE, a suicidal vampire begins a love affair with an old friend. When a terminal illness befalls her, he struggles to reconcile her remaining days with the curse of his infinitely mundane existence.


S H A R E        

Synopsis

The burden of Asian masculinity

AFTER NIGHTS OF passionless lovemaking, an Asian vampire and his wife repeat the same old fight. She wants kids. He doesn’t. Ever since settling in the prosperous city of Singapore, he has put his artist career on hold, turning to more pragmatic means of sustenance such as financial trading. Even as he struggles to generate income, he is unwilling to return to his art


Out of a masculine need to prove that he can provide financially.

As his trading losses mount, he grows increasingly pessimistic about life (in Singapore). And by extension, bringing life into this world. Unable to reconcile their procreational differences, he and his wife decide on a divorce.





SHORTLY AFTER, HE reconnects with an old female friend who also suffers from a broken marriage. The two confide in their shared pain, igniting dormant feelings they have had for one another over the years. As lust fans the flames of their passion, she becomes pregnant with his child, promptly followed by a miscarriage. The offspring of a human-vampire union rarely survives.

As she recovers from her miscarriage, she learns of an even worse predicament - terminal cancer. When her condition worsens and hospital stays become more frequent, she requests her lover to stay by her side in the hospital more often. Even during periods when the husband is absent, he is hesitant to be an outwardly caring lover.


The more time he spends with her, the greater his guilt towards her husband.

Believing that his blood may have the ability to save her, he performs an unorthodox blood transfusion. This appears to alleviate her symptoms temporarily but otherwise, has no impact on her recovery.




AFTER AN INTERMINABLY long hospital stay in which she fails to check in with him, he becomes distressed. Upon visiting her, he realizes to his horror that she no longer recognizes him. The cancer has spread so widely that part of her brain has shut down. Now, he truly regrets not spending more time with her prior. Unfortunately, it’s too little too late.

A few weeks later, she goes blind. A few days after that, she passes. On her deathbed, he decides to fulfill an unfulfilled request of hers -


To see the sunrise together.


S H A R E        

Director's Statement

An addiction to morals, pragmatism & responsibility

THE VAMPIRE AS antihero stems from my fascination with broken heroes. A hero with supernatural abilities. But not a superhero. Far from empowering him, his abilities burden, trap and imprison him in a state of moral, mental and emotional brokenness.

Even though he gets to live many human life times, his life experiences are limited to half a day every day because exposure to sunlight destroys him. In a way, he is half a human. So long as he stays in his bubble, he gets to live out a sheltered, mundane existence.


The vampire is a metaphor for the Singaporean dream.

Beyond a love story, this is a tale about the invisible societal forces that shape our gender roles and that prevents us from self-actualizing. This is not unlike The Lobster in which Colin Farrell and Rachel Weisz’s characters are constantly circumventing social norms in an attempt to discover their own brand of love. Like The Lobster, this film is


Magical realism. A tragicomedy. A social critique.


INSPIRED BY THE true story of a Singaporean male artist who refuses to depend on his female partner during times of financial hardship, he ends up giving up his art and eventually his marriage. To him, time not spent generating income or thinking about generating income is a sign of dependence. Of weakness. A sign that he isn’t fulfilling his responsibilities as a man. This is a film that


Brings into question the manly virtues of providership and self-sacrifice.

Whether such burden of responsibility is self-imposed or a result of living in Singapore is arguable. He becomes caught up in proving his own self worth via achievements in dollars and cents rather than the fulfillment he obtains from his art.

He sells his soul to the devil. To the rat race. To the superficial signs of accomplishment in Singapore. His fear of failure as an artist and the accompanying ridicule becomes so overwhelming that he’d rather not pursue his calling. In the end,


His only failure is that he fails to self-actualize.


THE MORAL CONUNDRUM of infidelity in the face of a terminal illness is another fascinating aspect of this story. Inspired by true events, the film explores the guilt the male lover harbours toward the husband. The greater his love for his lover, the greater his guilt toward the husband. Yet how can he possibly withhold love from a dying partner?

His moral dilemma stems from wanting to be a caring lover. As well as a righteous man.

He can be either. But he can’t be both. He leans toward being righteous only to regret when circumstances are too dire too late. But who’s to say there wouldn’t be worse repercussions if he acted less righteously?

This is a film that withholds judgments of its characters wherever possible. This allows audiences to live vicariously through the knotty situation, so that they may form their own conclusions and obtain solace should they one day find themselves in a similar predicament.
