Sheepwolf

An epic journey of survival from abuse, poverty & discrimination.

FOLLOWING HIS BROTHER'S murder, a real-life martial artist founds Singapore’s leading self-defense movement to empower women, least expecting the day he would have to protect his own daughter from her mom.

The screenplay is currently in development, slated for production in 2021/22.


S H A R E        

Synopsis

A journey in search of love & belonging.

SINGAPORE, 1970. TEN year old Chye huddles in a corner of his bed as he hears his abusive father beat his fourteen year old brother Chuan like a dog. Such is the fate of the two brothers, whose only misfortune is that their parents never planned to have them.


Same fate. Different destinies.

As the years go by, Chuan seeks solace in drugs, descending further into gangs and prisons while Chye discovers an alternative way out. All his life he’s been desperately trying to figure out why he’s not loved. This obsession eventually drives him to study psychology in Perth, Australia. After surviving a childhood of abuse, he now faces a different problem in a foreign land.


Being discriminated against as an immigrant.

To make ends meet, Chye takes on odd jobs involving hard labour which exposes him to racist attacks by bogans, Aussie rednecks. And on occasion, ostracized by his fellow wealthier Singaporean schoolmates. To fend off racist onslaughts and even a rape attempt by his professor, he learns various forms of martial arts. Despite a life of poverty and hardship, he excels in his studies and is offered a path to direct PhD.


He finally finds a sense of purpose and belonging.

Unfortunately, his mother’s suicide attempt due to prolonged abuse by his father causes him to withdraw from his PhD. He returns to Singapore for her well-being.




BY THE EARLY 90s, Chye transforms himself to a successful corporate man. Top of the food chain and surrounded by materialistic wealth, what he lacks is someone he could love and who loves him back. His inexperience in romance results in a grueling marriage that he attempts to salvage by having a child, Nicole. The marriage eventually ends with his ex-wife abandoning Chye and Nicole. As he showers unconditional love on Nicole, in return


He receives unconditional love for the first time in his life.

In 2000, tragedy strikes again. Fresh out of prison, Chye's older brother Chuan is savagely attacked in a street fight and lapses into a perpetual state of coma. Seeing his older brother in a vegetative state causes Chye to undergo what he calls a “crazy transformation.” Chye abandons his corporate job to dedicate his life to


A calling of teaching self-defense to save lives.


WHEN NICOLE TURNS eight in 2004, the unthinkable happens. Chye’s ex-wife returns to fight for custody of Nicole. She wins. As inconsolable grief besets Chye,


He is willing to lose everything to save the one person he can’t live without.

Which means exacting his own brand of justice. His lawyer dissuades him and they file an appeal in an attempt to reverse the verdict. Chye wins and is reunited with Nicole.

Today, Chye continues to enroll like-minded youths to his cause, spreading his mission to empower the average person, especially women with the skills necessary to protect their own lives.



S H A R E        

Director's Statement

A small epic film that can't be ignored.

WHERE MOST SINGAPOREAN films are contained in the context of Singapore culture, what drew me to Chye’s story was that it actively tests the Singaporean mentality when subjected to Western influence. Not unlike Middle Eastern and Asian immigrants who settle in western cultures. But unlike them because in Sheepwolf, Chye is the bearer of western ideology when he returns to his home country.

After completing my very first draft of Sheepwolf, then known as Of Meek and Menace, I showed it to close friends to elicit feedback. What I learned from long time friend Arlene startled me. She observed that,


Chye’s story was in fact my story.

She was right. I identified closely with Chye’s struggles as an Asian student living in a Western society during my studies overseas. His divorce also struck a chord with me. I happened to be going through my own divorce during the period I penned the screenplay.




JUST AS ANG Lee benefited from having his American producer James Schamus interpret Crouching Tiger Hidden Dragon from a Westerner’s perspective, I invited Arlene to co-write Sheepwolf. This meant restarting the writing from scratch. A complete rewrite. Which I welcomed with open arms.

Together we strived to create a prestige drama that was more accessible to an international audience while developing elements that made it an authentic Singaporean film. Contradictory as it may seem, the multicultural quality of this story lends itself perfectly to


A social critique of Asian vs Western values.

The previous century has seen the Asian male being typecast in mainstream English language films as either nerds, wimps or evildoers. But showing Asian males in an empowering light shouldn't require defaulting to white machismo. More specifically, toxic American masculinity. Where the West is about individual over society, Asian cultures are about society over individual. The Asian male exposed to Western culture is caught between these two states of being. Hence,

Portraying the Asian male as emotionally complex is our response to toxic masculinity.


AS WE DELVED deep into Chye’s life in Australia and how that impacted his decisions and values after his return to Singapore, it struck me that this is a highly relevant film for Asian parents hoping to send their children overseas for studies.

Such a film had not been made. Something deep within me was crying out for it to be made.


It was going to be the small epic film that can’t be ignored.

Epic because the story spans four decades with consistent themes that ripple throughout Chye’s life. Also because audiences can bear witness to the toll that the abuse Chye received as a child has on his adulthood. Small because Sheepwolf is ultimately


An intimate family drama about the search for love and belonging.

Amidst the chronic anger, fear and betrayal that Chye harboured throughout his life, love and a sense of belonging was the Utopia that he constantly yearned for. That constantly eluded him every time he believes he has found it.

I hope the fact that Sheepwolf is based on a true story gives the film the necessary gravitas for audiences to take the social issues in the film seriously and to allow themselves to be transformed by it.
